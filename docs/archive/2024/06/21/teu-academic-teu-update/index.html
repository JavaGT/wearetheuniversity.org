Health, safety, and wellbeing. Last week we reported that we declined to immediately sign off on the ACC audit request, on account of the perceived gaps in wellbeing protections. We advised HR of this. We received a response on the same day, pointing out that an inability to audit would result in reversion to ACC coverage which is problematic in the current political environment (see, e.g., herehttps://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/520003/acc-taken-to-court-as-claimants-lose-jobs-homes-while-waiting-for-disputes-to-be-reviewed and herehttps://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/518563/acc-levies-may-increase-due-to-rise-in-serious-injuries-rehabilitation-costs). We signed but sought assurances that we will get answers to our questions this week re:

  • the status of the bargaining-mandated, joint TEU-HR working group on workload and wellbeing;
  • TEU and wider staff involvement in policy development that affects our wellbeing; and
  • the collection and analysis of statistics on the broad range of factors that impact staff wellbeing, including but not limited to work-related stress and leave, threats, bullying, harassment, and discrimination. We have also asked to meet with the ACC auditors when they are on campus to discuss.

Massey U’s intrusion into academic freedom. Clause 8 of the proposed Academic Freedom policy here states: “The exercise of academic freedom is also subject to the constraints imposed by the University by way of its reasonable requirements to:

  • regulate the subject matter of courses taught at the University;
  • teach and assess students in the way that it considers best promotes learning…” Massey U has begun to reveal how this might be done: https://www.stuff.co.nz/nz-news/350317168/massey-academics-fear-loss-curriculum-ownership, prompting some academic staff to raise concerns with how decisions about content and assessment could be taken away from them and be assumed by a qualification committee. TEU member Bryan Walpert said it was a worrying shift of autonomy away from experts: “I’m going to have to negotiate with a learning specialist about my content and my pedagogy. That’s outrageous.”

  • Bargaining 101: What is Collective Bargaining? Collective Bargaining is the way that members, through their union representatives, negotiate their collective agreement with their employers to determine their pay, terms and conditions of employment. Terms of employment include things like hours of work, annual and sick leave and the way job progression works. In collective bargaining both union members and the employer bring claims to the other party of what changes they would like to see in the renewed collective agreement. You can find the current Collective Agreements at Waipapa Taumata Rau/ UoA at https://uoacollectiveagreements.blogs.auckland.ac.nz/collective-employment-agreements/https://url.au.m.mimecastprotect.com/s/dYMtCzvk8xtyQ7XAh4etZU?domain=uoacollectiveagreements.blogs.auckland.ac.nz/.
