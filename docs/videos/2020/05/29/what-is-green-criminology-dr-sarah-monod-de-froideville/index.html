My name is Sarah Monod de Froideville.
I'm a green criminologist with the Institute
of Criminology at the School of Social and
Cultural Studies.
I grew up on the west coast of the South Island,
on a lifestyle block, I guess.
And my parents were hippies, for a lack of
a better word.
So I was the kid that went to school with
the brown bread sandwiches with bean sprouts
and I hated it.
I really rejected the whole organic, green
kind of concept for a long long time, now
I'm back to it, back to my bean sprouts.
Green criminology is the study of environmental
harm and it's taking an eco-justice approach.
There's three ways you can look at environmental
harm: through a legal lens, an ecological
lens, a justice lens.
By thinking about rights, so the rights of
people to have access to environmental resources,
the rights of animals to live as sentient
beings, and the rights of ecosystems to remain
undisturbed by human activity.
The change that I see in the students that
I teach is that they are a lot more aware
of how their lives and what they do contributes
to environmental harm.
Part of our job as green criminologists is
making harm visible so that other people can
become more aware of how they affect the environment.
