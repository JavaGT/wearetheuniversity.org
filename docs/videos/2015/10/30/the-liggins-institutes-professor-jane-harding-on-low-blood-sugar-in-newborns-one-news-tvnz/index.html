Description

Treating blood sugar levels in newborns with hypoglycemia, or low blood sugar, appears to prevent brain damage. That’s according to a study led by the Liggins Institute at the University of Auckland in New Zealand, and published in The New England Journal of Medicine. Distinguished Professor Jane Harding explains to ONE News Health Reporter Lorelei Mason that if doctors treat a baby with hypoglycemia to keep the blood sugar above a safety threshold, there is no increase in the risk of brain damage. Hypoglycemia is the single most preventable cause of brain damage in newborns. Up to 30 per cent of newborns are at risk, 15 per cent will be affected to at least some degree, and around 10 per cent end up admitted to intensive care.
